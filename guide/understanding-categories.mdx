title: Understanding categories

---

Cite Unseen automatically analyzes citations on Wikipedia articles and marks them with categorical icons. These categories help you quickly evaluate the reliability and type of sources used in articles.

## Reliability categories

Reliability categories are based on community consensus documented on various Wikipedia reliability project pages. These categories indicate the community's assessment of a source's trustworthiness.

<Accordion title="Blacklisted">
Sources that have been blacklisted due to persistent abuse, typically in the form of spam external links.

**Icon color:** Red circle with X

**What this means:** These sources should not be used and may be automatically blocked from Wikipedia.

**Example sources:** Domains that have been blocked site-wide for spam or abuse.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Deprecated">
Sources that are deprecated and should not be used. They may still be acceptable for non-controversial self-descriptions or expert self-published content.

**Icon color:** Red/orange warning triangle

**What this means:** Avoid using these sources except in very specific circumstances.

**Example sources:** Outlets that have lost credibility due to editorial failures or persistent reliability issues.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Generally unreliable">
Sources that are generally considered unreliable by the community. They may still be used for non-controversial self-descriptions or expert self-published content.

**Icon color:** Red X

**What this means:** These sources lack editorial oversight, fact-checking, or have a history of publishing inaccurate information.

**Common categories:**
- News aggregators without original reporting
- Publications with poor fact-checking records
- Sources with significant conflicts of interest
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Marginally reliable">
Sources with mixed reliability that need to be reviewed on a case-by-case basis to determine reliability in each context.

**Icon color:** Yellow warning triangle

**What this means:** Use caution. These sources may be reliable for some topics but not others.

**Example situations:**
- Video game news sites (reliable for gaming topics, not for general news)
- Trade publications (reliable within their industry)
- Regional newspapers (reliable for local coverage)
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="No consensus">
Sources where there is no consensus in the community about reliability. Reliability may depend on subject area, author, or publication time.

**Icon color:** Blue circle with question mark

**What this means:** You need to evaluate the specific use case and consider factors like the topic, author credentials, and context.

**Factors affecting reliability:**
- Subject matter expertise
- Individual author qualifications
- Time period of publication
- Type of content (news vs. opinion vs. analysis)
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Generally reliable">
Sources that editors generally agree are reliable on topics in their area of expertise.

**Icon color:** Green checkmark

**What this means:** These sources have editorial oversight, fact-checking processes, and a reputation for accuracy.

**Common characteristics:**
- Professional editorial staff
- Fact-checking procedures
- Corrections policy
- Reputation for accuracy
</Accordion>

## Type categories

Type categories describe the nature or format of a source rather than its reliability. A source can have multiple type categories.

### Content format types

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="News">
  Sources from outlets whose primary purpose is journalistic reporting on current/recent events, under an editorial process.

  **Icon:** Newspaper

  **Examples from sources.js:**
  - Major newspapers (The New York Times, The Guardian)
  - News agencies (Associated Press, Reuters)
  - Online news outlets (BBC News, CNN)

  <Note>A news designation doesn't automatically mean reliability. Check reliability categories separately.</Note>
  </Tab>

  <Tab title="Books">
  Publications such as books, journals, or other printed material.

  **Icon:** Book

  **Detection:** Cite Unseen identifies these through CSS classes like `book`, `journal`, `encyclopaedia`, `conference`, `thesis`, or `magazine`.

  **Examples:**
  - Academic journals
  - Published books
  - Conference proceedings
  - Theses and dissertations
  </Tab>

  <Tab title="Blogs">
  Blog posts and personal websites.

  **Icon:** Pencil/writing symbol

  **Common sources:** Personal blogs, Medium posts, Substack newsletters

  <Warning>Blog posts are typically considered self-published sources and may not be reliable unless written by recognized experts.</Warning>
  </Tab>

  <Tab title="Social media">
  Social media websites, possibly including individual social media posts.

  **Icon:** Network/share symbol

  **Examples from sources.js:**
  - Twitter/X posts
  - Facebook posts
  - Instagram content
  - TikTok videos
  - Reddit threads

  <Warning>Social media posts are generally not considered reliable sources except for direct quotes or verified statements by notable individuals.</Warning>
  </Tab>

  <Tab title="TV programs">
  TV or radio programs. Reliability depends on the individual program.

  **Icon:** Television

  **Detection:** Identified through CSS classes like `episode`, `podcast`, or `media`.

  **Examples:**
  - Television news programs
  - Documentaries
  - Radio shows
  - Podcasts
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

### Special content types

<Accordion title="Opinions">
Opinion pieces, editorials, and columns.

**Icon:** Speech bubble

**What this means:** The content represents the author's opinion rather than factual reporting. Even from reliable sources, opinions should be attributed to the author.

**Usage:** Can be used for attributed opinions but not for stating facts.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Press releases">
Official press releases from organizations.

**Icon:** Megaphone

**Detection:** Identified through the CSS class `pressrelease`.

<Warning>Press releases are promotional material and should be used cautiously. They're acceptable for basic facts about the issuing organization but not for independent verification.</Warning>
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Sponsored content">
Sponsored content or promotional material.

**Icon:** Dollar sign

**What this means:** The content was paid for by an advertiser and may not maintain editorial independence.

<Warning>Sponsored content should generally not be used as a source for Wikipedia content.</Warning>
</Accordion>

### Concern categories

<Accordion title="Editable sources">
Sources that are editable by the community (e.g., wikis or databases).

**Icon:** Pencil with page

**Examples:**
- Other wikis
- User-editable databases
- Collaborative platforms

<Warning>Editable sources lack editorial control and can be changed by anyone. They're generally not acceptable as reliable sources.</Warning>
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="AI-generated content">
Sources associated with unedited AI-generated content. This may include websites that publish substantial AI-generated material without human editorial oversight.

**Icon:** Robot/AI symbol

**What this means:** The content may be generated by AI without adequate human review, which can lead to factual errors or "hallucinations."

<Warning>AI-generated content without editorial oversight is not considered a reliable source.</Warning>
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="AI-referred">
The URL includes tracking parameters (such as `utm_source=chatgpt.com`) indicating it was copied from an AI assistant.

**Icon:** Robot with link symbol

**What this means:** The source was obtained through an AI assistant. The associated text may also be AI-generated, and the source may or may not corroborate the given statements.

<Info>This doesn't necessarily mean the source itself is unreliable, but it flags that extra verification may be needed.</Info>
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Advocacy organizations">
Sources from advocacy organizations.

**Icon:** Raised fist or banner

**What this means:** The source has a specific viewpoint or agenda they're promoting.

**Usage considerations:**
- Can be used for the organization's own positions
- Should not be used as independent verification
- May lack neutrality on related topics
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Government sources">
State-owned or state-run media, or government sources.

**Icon:** Government building

**What this means:** The source is controlled or funded by a government, which may affect editorial independence.

**Usage:** Acceptable for official government positions and statistics, but consider potential bias on politically sensitive topics.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Predatory journals">
Sources from predatory journals.

**Icon:** Warning symbol with document

**What this means:** These are academic-looking journals that lack proper peer review and publish articles for profit without maintaining quality standards.

<Warning>Predatory journals should not be used as sources. They lack the editorial oversight expected of academic publications.</Warning>
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Tabloids">
Tabloid or gossip news.

**Icon:** Sensational newspaper

**What this means:** These publications prioritize entertainment over accuracy and often publish unverified claims.

**Examples:** Celebrity gossip magazines, sensationalist newspapers

<Warning>Tabloids are generally unreliable and should be avoided except for information about themselves.</Warning>
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Satire">
Sources that publish satirical or parody content.

**Icon:** Comedy masks

**What this means:** The content is intentionally fictional or exaggerated for humor.

**Examples:** The Onion, The Borowitz Report

<Warning>Satirical sources should never be used for factual claims. They can only be used when discussing the satire itself.</Warning>
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Community news">
Community-created news.

**Icon:** Group of people

**What this means:** News content created by community members rather than professional journalists.

**Examples:** Citizen journalism platforms, community news sites

**Reliability:** Varies widely depending on editorial oversight and verification processes.
</Accordion>

## Unknown sources

Citations that Cite Unseen cannot categorize are marked as "unknown." This doesn't mean the source is unreliable—it simply means it hasn't been evaluated yet.

**Icon:** Question mark

**What to do:** You'll need to manually evaluate these sources using Wikipedia's reliability guidelines. Consider suggesting categorization to help improve Cite Unseen's database.

## Multiple categories

A citation can have multiple categories. For example, a blog post from a news website might show both "news" and "blogs" icons. A social media post from a deprecated source might show both "social" and "deprecated" icons.

<Tip>When a citation has multiple categories, pay special attention to any reliability indicators (red, yellow, or green icons) as these are the most important for evaluating source quality.</Tip>

## Language indicators

When a reliability rating comes from a different language Wikipedia's source evaluation project, you'll see a language code (like "EN" or "ZH") next to the reliability icon. This indicates which Wikipedia community made the reliability assessment.

**Why this matters:** Different language Wikipedias may have different perspectives on source reliability, especially for regional sources.
