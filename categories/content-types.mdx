---
title: Content Types
description: Categories identifying the format and medium of cited sources
---

Content type categories help identify the format of cited sources. These categories describe what kind of content you're looking at, independent of its reliability.

## News

<Accordion title="About News Sources">
This source is from an outlet whose primary purpose is journalistic reporting on current/recent events, under an editorial process.

News sources follow journalistic standards and editorial processes. The reliability of individual news sources varies and is tracked separately through reliability ratings.
</Accordion>

**Icon:** Newspaper symbol

## Blogs

<Accordion title="About Blog Sources">
This source is a blog post.

Blogs are typically personal or informal publications. Unless written by recognized experts, blogs are generally considered self-published sources and may have reliability concerns.
</Accordion>

**Icon:** Blog/document symbol

## Books

<Accordion title="About Book Sources">
This source is a publication such as a book, journal, or other printed material.

Books and printed publications vary in reliability based on the publisher, author credentials, and editorial oversight. Academic and scholarly books from reputable publishers are generally reliable.
</Accordion>

**Icon:** Book symbol

## Opinions

<Accordion title="About Opinion Pieces">
This source is an opinion piece.

Opinion pieces, editorials, and op-eds represent the author's views rather than factual reporting. They should be attributed to the author and used carefully, particularly for controversial topics.
</Accordion>

**Icon:** Speech bubble or opinion symbol

## Press Releases

<Accordion title="About Press Releases">
This source is a press release.

Press releases are promotional materials from organizations. They are primary sources about the organization itself but should not be used as independent verification of claims.
</Accordion>

**Icon:** Megaphone or announcement symbol

## Social Media

<Accordion title="About Social Media Sources">
This source is a social media website, possibly a social media post.

Social media posts are generally not reliable sources unless from verified accounts of recognized experts or official organizational accounts. Use with caution and proper attribution.
</Accordion>

**Icon:** Network/social symbol

## TV Programs

<Accordion title="About TV Program Sources">
This source is a TV or radio program. Its reliability depends on the individual program.

Television and radio programs vary widely in reliability. News programs from reputable broadcasters are generally reliable, while entertainment programs are not.
</Accordion>

**Icon:** Television symbol

## Tabloids

<Accordion title="About Tabloid Sources">
This source is a tabloid or gossip news.

Tableids focus on sensationalism and entertainment rather than factual reporting. They are generally considered unreliable sources for Wikipedia content.
</Accordion>

**Icon:** Tabloid newspaper symbol

## Community

<Accordion title="About Community News Sources">
This source is community-created news.

Community-created news sources involve contributions from community members rather than professional journalists. Reliability varies based on editorial oversight.
</Accordion>

**Icon:** Community/group symbol
