Reliability ratings indicate the Wikipedia community's consensus about a source's trustworthiness. These ratings come from various Wikipedia reliability project pages and perennial source lists.

## Blacklisted

<Accordion title="About Blacklisted Sources">
This source has been blacklisted due to persistent abuse, typically in the form of spam external links.

Blacklisted sources should not be used in Wikipedia articles under any circumstances. The blacklist is maintained to prevent spam and abuse.
</Accordion>

**Icon:** Red stop sign

**Sources:** Wikipedia perennial sources lists, Korea sources, vaccine safety sources, Vietnamese reliable sources

## Deprecated

<Accordion title="About Deprecated Sources">
This source is deprecated and should not be used. It may still be used for non-controversial self-descriptions or expert self-published content.

Deprecated sources have been determined to be unreliable or problematic for general use. Use extreme caution and only in limited circumstances with strong justification.
</Accordion>

**Icon:** Red warning triangle

**Sources:** Wikipedia perennial sources lists, record charts

## Generally Unreliable

<Accordion title="About Generally Unreliable Sources">
This source is generally considered unreliable by the community. It may still be used for non-controversial self-descriptions or expert self-published content.

Generally unreliable sources lack editorial oversight, fact-checking, or have a history of publishing inaccurate information. They should be avoided for most Wikipedia content.
</Accordion>

## Marginally Reliable

<Accordion title="About Marginally Reliable Sources">
This source is marginally reliable. It may be necessary to review it on a case-by-case basis to determine its reliability in each context.

Marginally reliable sources have some editorial oversight but may have issues with accuracy, bias, or expertise in certain topics. Exercise caution and verify information with better sources when possible.
</Accordion>

**Icon:** Yellow warning sign

**Sources:** Multiple Wikipedia reliability projects including perennial sources, anime and manga, Japan, Korea, video games, board games, vaccine safety, French video games, Vietnamese reliable sources, and Chinese ACG/video game sources

## No Consensus

<Accordion title="About No Consensus Sources">
There is no consensus in the community about the reliability of this source. Its reliability may be affected by one or more factors, such as the subject area, author, or publication time.

Sources without consensus require careful evaluation on a case-by-case basis. The reliability may depend on the specific article, author, topic, or time period.
</Accordion>

**Icon:** Blue question mark

**Sources:** Wikipedia perennial sources, new pages patrol guide, video games, vaccine safety, Vietnamese reliable sources, Chinese ACG/video game sources

## Generally Reliable

<Accordion title="About Generally Reliable Sources">
Editors generally agree that this source is reliable on topics in its area of expertise.

Generally reliable sources have strong editorial oversight, fact-checking processes, and a reputation for accuracy. They are preferred for Wikipedia content.
</Accordion>

**Icon:** Green checkmark

**Sources:** Multiple Wikipedia reliability projects including perennial sources, anime and manga, albums, Japan, Korea, new pages patrol, video games, board games, record charts, vaccine safety, film resources, French video games, Russian computer games, Vietnamese reliable sources, and Chinese ACG/video game sources
